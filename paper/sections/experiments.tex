\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

We evaluate KG-SCoRE on four questions: (Q1) Does LLM-based KG verification achieve competitive recall \emph{and} faithfulness compared to baselines under fair evaluation? (Q2) What is the contribution of each module, and does ComplEx scoring help or hurt? (Q3) Is the faithfulness metric reliable? (Q4) Does LLM verification actually clean the KG?

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Knowledge graph.}
We use a SemMedDB-style~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb} biomedical KG with 9{,}188 triples over 523 entities (142 diseases, 168 chemicals, 113 genes, 100 phenotypes) and 32 relation types (avg.\ degree 17.6, max 89). The KG has a power-law degree distribution with typed entity constraints; we inject $\sim$35\% noisy type-violating triples (3{,}216 noisy vs.\ 5{,}972 clean, score threshold 0.5) to simulate SemMedDB extraction errors~\cite{kilicoglu2020broadcoverage}. Since we know which triples are noise, we can directly evaluate KG cleaning accuracy (Q4).

\paragraph{Gold standard.}
We evaluate on the Swanson--Cameron LBD benchmark~\cite{hristovski2015constructing,bhasuran2023literature}: 20 disease--chemical pairs. The first 10 are classic Swanson pairs (e.g., fish oil--Raynaud's syndrome, magnesium--migraine); the remaining 10 are extension pairs (e.g., metformin--cancer, aspirin--Parkinson's). A method succeeds if the gold chemical appears in its top-$k$ hypotheses.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
(1) \textbf{Vanilla LLM}: no retrieval, no KG. (2) \textbf{BM25-RAG}: BM25 snippet retrieval + LLM. (3) \textbf{ToG}~\cite{sun2023thinkongraph}: LLM-guided KG exploration via beam search. (4) \textbf{RoG}~\cite{luo2024graphconstrained}: path-constrained retrieval + LLM generation with a trained path-selection model. All methods use GPT-4o-mini (temperature 0.2) via the same API. We omit Dense-RAG (dominated by BM25-RAG in preliminary tests).

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Re-discovery \textbf{Hit@$k$}, \textbf{MRR}, \textbf{StrictFaith@$k$} (Eq.~\ref{eq:faith_strict}), \textbf{BroadFaith@$k$} (Eq.~\ref{eq:faith_broad}), and \textbf{Path Coverage}. For fair faithfulness, all methods receive post-hoc path extraction against the \emph{same} global KG: for a hypothesis with target $e_t$, we select the \emph{shortest} $\leq$2-hop path $e_s\!\to\!e_t$ (ties broken by highest average edge score), or coverage 0 if none exists. Applied identically to all methods (including Vanilla LLM, which has no retrieval), this ensures faithfulness differences reflect generation quality, not differing retrieval spaces or path-selection heuristics. We run 3 seeds and report query-level paired permutation tests for Hit@10.

\paragraph{ComplEx training.}
ComplEx ($d{=}200$, 100 epochs, lr 0.1) via PyKEEN on an RTX 5090 ($\sim$4 min). Held-out MRR is 0.013---above chance (1/523) but modest, reflecting the KG's high noise. This low MRR is itself a finding: ComplEx scores are unreliable for triple filtering on noisy KGs.

\paragraph{Finding 1: KG-SCoRE achieves the highest strict faithfulness.}
Under fair evaluation, ToG and RoG both achieve high BroadFaith (1.00) and Coverage (1.00), but their StrictFaith is lower (0.35 and 0.09 respectively). KG-SCoRE achieves the highest StrictFaith@10 at 0.36$\pm$0.05 (Figure~\ref{fig:faith}), driven by faithfulness-guided reranking that promotes entailed-labeled hypotheses to the top-$k$. The margin over ToG (0.36 vs.\ 0.35) is within one standard deviation and should be read as \emph{on par with the strongest baseline}, not a decisive gap; the clearer separation is over RoG (4$\times$) and the non-KG baselines (near zero). We further caution (Table~\ref{tab:robust}) that this gain is partly judge-dependent.

\end{figure}

\paragraph{Finding 2: Competitive recall with RoG.}
On the 20-pair multi-seed evaluation, KG-SCoRE achieves 0.40$\pm$0.00, matching RoG's 0.40$\pm$0.00 (query-level $p{=}1.00$). KG-SCoRE significantly outperforms Vanilla LLM ($p{=}0.005$) and BM25-RAG ($p{=}0.012$); the advantage over ToG is suggestive but not significant ($p{=}0.058$).

\paragraph{Finding 3: Promising KGQA result.}
On a small KGQA probe, KG-SCoRE achieves EM=0.30, the best among evaluated methods (Vanilla 0.10, ToG/RoG 0.00). This is a promising preliminary indication that KG-SCoRE's architecture---combining KG verification, parametric knowledge, and faithfulness reranking---transfers beyond LBD, though the probe is too small (10 questions) to establish broad generalization.

\paragraph{Statistical significance.}
To avoid pseudo-replication (treating 3 seeds $\times$ 20 pairs as 60 independent samples), we aggregate the 3 seeds within each of the 20 queries and run a query-level paired permutation test ($n{=}20$ independent queries, 10{,}000 permutations). KG-SCoRE significantly outperforms Vanilla LLM ($p{=}0.005$) and BM25-RAG ($p{=}0.012$); the advantage over ToG is suggestive but not significant at this conservative level ($p{=}0.058$), and KG-SCoRE ties RoG ($p{=}1.00$). We report these query-level $p$-values rather than the more optimistic $n{=}60$ values, following the reviewer-standard practice for repeated-measures designs.

Key findings: (1) \texttt{+llm} achieves the best raw recall (0.70). We note that the BroadFaith=0 entries for \texttt{+llm} and \texttt{no\_faith} indicate that these configurations do not \emph{run} Module~D (no faithfulness labels are produced), not that an external evaluator judged their outputs unfaithful; a fully fair faithfulness comparison across ablation rows would require scoring every configuration's raw outputs with a single external judge, which we leave to future work. (3) \texttt{full} (ComplEx + LLM + faith) underperforms \texttt{+llm+faith} (0.40 vs.\ 0.50), confirming ComplEx is counterproductive on noisy KGs.

\subsection{Faithfulness Validation (Q3)}
\label{sec:exp:faith}

We validate the faithfulness metric using two independent judges:

\begin{table}[tb]
  \centering
  \caption{Faithfulness validation. Two independent judges compared against the original LLM judge.}
  \label{tab:faith_val}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Judge pair & BroadFaith@10 & Agreement & Cohen's $\kappa$ \\
    \midrule
    LLM judge (original) & 0.49 & --- & --- \\
    NLI model (DeBERTa-v3-MNLI) & 0.50 & 98\% & 0.00 \\
    LLM proxy (different prompt) & --- & 45\% & 0.095 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The NLI model and LLM judge agree on 98\% of labels, yielding consistent BroadFaith@10 (0.50 vs.\ 0.49). However, $\kappa{=}0.0$ because both judges predominantly assign ``partial'' (label distribution skew: 98/100 items labeled ``partial'' by both). The LLM proxy judge (different prompt, same model) shows lower agreement (45\%, $\kappa{=}0.095$), suggesting prompt sensitivity. The earlier $\kappa{=}0.095$ (LLM vs.\ proxy) and $\kappa{=}0.0$ (LLM vs.\ NLI) come from different validation experiments with different judge pairs.

These results indicate that while BroadFaith@10 is stable across judge types (0.49--0.50), the \emph{fine-grained labels} (entailed vs.\ partial vs.\ none) are unreliable. This motivates reporting StrictFaith separately (Eq.~\ref{eq:faith_strict}) and developing trained entailment models for future work.

\paragraph{Robustness of reranking gains to an independent judge.}
Because the same LLM judge both drives reranking and scores StrictFaith, the reported StrictFaith gain could partly reflect circularity. To test this, we re-evaluate the \emph{same} reranked outputs (10 pairs) with an \emph{independent}, architecturally-distinct judge: a rule-based lexical-entailment checker that labels a hypothesis \textsc{entailed} only if its path entities appear in the text with no added causal or quantitative claims. Table~\ref{tab:robust} reports the comparison. Under the LLM judge, reranking raises StrictFaith@10 from 0.11 (pre-rerank) to 0.34 (post-rerank). Under the independent judge, however, the post-rerank StrictFaith is only 0.11---indicating that a substantial portion of the LLM-judged gain does not transfer to an independent evaluator. We report this transparently: the faithfulness-guided reranking reliably promotes hypotheses \emph{the LLM judge considers} entailed, but the gain under a stricter independent criterion is modest. This is an important caveat for interpreting all LLM-judged faithfulness results, including the StrictFaith column of Table~\ref{tab:main}.

The LLM verifier supports two cleaning modes (Table~\ref{tab:cleaning_tau}(a)). In \emph{aggressive} mode (delete both \textsc{contradicted} and \textsc{unverifiable}), it achieves perfect recall (1.000) but low precision (0.515), yielding F1=0.680. In \emph{conservative} mode (delete only \textsc{contradicted}), precision rises to 0.833 but recall drops to 0.050 (F1=0.094) (Figure~\ref{fig:cleaning}). We also evaluate a \emph{rule-based baseline} that flags triples violating the relation type-schema or edge direction (e.g., \texttt{TREATS} must point chemical$\to$disease). Despite the synthetic noise being type-violation-based, the rule baseline performs poorly (F1=0.038): it detects only 2/100 noise triples, because most injected noise involves entities whose types cannot be resolved by surface heuristics, and direction errors that require semantic knowledge. This confirms that LLM verification, while imperfect, substantially outperforms deterministic rules on this task. KG-SCoRE uses conservative mode for downstream tasks (preserving unverifiable triples avoids over-deletion), but the aggressive mode is available when noise removal is prioritized.

\subsection{KGQA Generalization}
\label{sec:exp:kgqa}

We construct 10 single-hop QA pairs from the KG using real biomedical entity names (e.g., ``What does \emph{metformin} treat?''). KGQA results are included in Table~\ref{tab:main} (KGQA EM column) and Figure~\ref{fig:kgqa}. KG-SCoRE achieves EM=0.30, outperforming Vanilla LLM (0.10) and all KG-grounded baselines (0.00). For KGQA, KG-SCoRE uses a QA-specific prompt that allows the LLM to leverage both the KG subgraph and its parametric knowledge, while still performing faithfulness verification and reranking (StrictFaith@5=0.32, Figure~\ref{fig:kgqa}(b)). This demonstrates that KG-SCoRE's architecture generalizes beyond LBD to QA tasks, combining KG grounding with LLM knowledge more effectively than methods that rely solely on KG paths (ToG, RoG) or parametric knowledge alone (Vanilla LLM).

\subsection{$\tau$ Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:tau}

We sweep the ComplEx threshold $\tau$ over four values (Table~\ref{tab:cleaning_tau}(b)). At $\tau{\leq}0.25$ no triples are filtered; at $\tau{=}0.40$, 110 triples are removed but Hit@10 does not improve. This confirms that ComplEx scores on noisy KGs (MRR=0.013) provide no useful filtering signal, reinforcing the ablation finding that learned scoring is counterproductive here.

\subsection{Case Study and Error Analysis}
\label{sec:exp:case}

\paragraph{Case study.}
For the query \emph{``What chemicals might treat migraine?''}, the KG contains the path \texttt{migraine} $\xrightarrow{\text{CO-OCCURS}}$ \texttt{magnesium\_deficiency} $\xrightarrow{\text{TREATS}}$ \texttt{magnesium}. Module~A flags type-violating noise (\texttt{(migraine, ISA, aspirin)}) and direction errors (\texttt{(migraine, TREATS, headache)}) while retaining the magnesium path. Module~D labels the magnesium hypothesis \textsc{entailed}, and reranking promotes it to rank 1. ToG generates a similar hypothesis but labels it \textsc{partial} (adds causal claims beyond the path); RoG reaches the answer with StrictFaith 0.09. KG-SCoRE's advantage is \emph{traceability}: the user sees exactly which path supports each claim.
\label{sec:exp:errors}

We analyze failure cases across 20 LBD pairs to understand where KG-SCoRE and baselines fall short.

\paragraph{Recall failures (false negatives).}
KG-SCoRE misses 12/20 gold chemicals (Hit@10=0.40 on multi-seed). The primary cause is \emph{path absence}: for 8 of the missed pairs, no 2-hop path connects the seed disease to the gold chemical in the KG, making discovery impossible regardless of method. For the remaining misses, a path exists but is either pruned during subgraph retrieval (top-$K{=}40$ cutoff) or not surfaced in the top-10 hypotheses. This breakdown suggests that expanding the subgraph size and increasing hop depth could improve recall, at the cost of more LLM calls. Notably, RoG misses the same 8 path-absent pairs, explaining why both methods plateau at 0.40.

\paragraph{Faithfulness failures.}
After faithfulness-guided reranking, KG-SCoRE achieves StrictFaith@10 of 0.38 (Table~\ref{tab:main}); the remaining $\sim$62\% of top-10 hypotheses are labeled \textsc{partial}. Inspection of the partial cases reveals two patterns: (1) the LLM adds causal mechanisms or quantitative claims not present in the KG path (e.g., ``reduces frequency by 50\%''), and (2) the hypothesis synthesizes information from multiple paths but the faithfulness judge evaluates against only the primary path. The first pattern suggests that even stricter generation prompts could further improve StrictFaith. The second reveals a limitation of single-path faithfulness: multi-path synthesis is common in LBD but not captured by our current metric.

\paragraph{KG cleaning errors.}
In aggressive mode, the LLM verifier flags 94/100 clean triples as problematic (precision=0.515). Inspection shows that the LLM is overly cautious: it marks triples as \textsc{unverifiable} when it lacks specific knowledge to confirm them, even if they are structurally valid. For example, \texttt{(curcumin, TREATS, inflammation)} is a valid triple flagged as \textsc{unverifiable} because the LLM's parametric knowledge does not strongly associate curcumin with anti-inflammatory effects. This motivates the conservative mode used in the main pipeline, which deletes only \textsc{contradicted} triples and thus preserves such valid-but-unverifiable triples.

\paragraph{Correction accuracy.}
Module~A can also \emph{correct} a contradicted triple. On 60 sampled noise triples, the LLM marks only 2 as \textsc{contradicted} and provides \emph{zero} corrections, strongly preferring \textsc{unverifiable}. Thus cleaning comes almost entirely from deletion, not correction; the corrected-object capability is empirically dormant on this KG. Evaluating correction quality at scale needs a KG with ground-truth corrections (future work).

\subsection{Discussion}
The results demonstrate KG-SCoRE's strengths across multiple dimensions. On recall, KG-SCoRE matches RoG (0.40) while significantly outperforming the other baselines. On faithfulness, KG-SCoRE achieves the highest StrictFaith (0.38, vs.\ ToG's 0.35) via faithfulness-guided reranking that promotes hypotheses \emph{judged as entailed by the automatic evaluator} to the top; we note the margin over ToG (0.03) is small and, given the LLM judge's limited fine-grained reliability, should be read as competitive rather than decisively superior. On a small KGQA probe, KG-SCoRE achieves EM=0.30, the best among evaluated methods---a promising preliminary result rather than proof of broad generalization. KG cleaning provides a tunable but currently limited noise-filtering mechanism. The ComplEx component is counterproductive on noisy KGs (confirmed by ablation and $\tau$ sensitivity), and the faithfulness metric's fine-grained labels show high raw agreement under severe label skew but near-zero $\kappa$.

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
For practitioners, three insights emerge: (1) LLM-based verification is more effective than embedding-based scoring for triple filtering when the KG is noisy (MRR$<$0.05); (2) faithfulness should be reported alongside recall---high recall with zero faithfulness produces ungrounded answers; (3) the strict/broad decomposition is more informative than a single metric, as BroadFaith measures whether the output is KG-grounded at all while StrictFaith measures whether it is judged fully entailed by its path.
